# Website Quality Guidelines

This document defines what would make The Invention Club website good from three angles:

- aesthetic quality;
- practical usability;
- brand consistency.

It is written as an evaluation tool for this prototype, not as a production design system.

## Definition Of Good

The website is good if a parent can quickly understand, trust and remember the idea:

> The Invention Club is a hands-on creative engineering club where children learn to invent, not just code.

A good version should feel:

- playful but credible;
- bright but not babyish;
- tactile and workshop-like;
- structured without feeling schoolish;
- parent-friendly rather than curriculum-designer-facing.

Use the scoring model below when reviewing a version of the site.

| Area | Weight | Good score |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Aesthetic quality | 35 | 28+ |
| Practical usability | 40 | 32+ |
| Brand consistency | 25 | 20+ |
| Total | 100 | 80+ |

A version should not be considered good if it fails any must-pass gate, even if the total score is high.

## Must-Pass Gates

These are binary checks.

| Gate | Pass condition |
| --- | --- |
| First-screen clarity | Within the first screen, a parent can tell this is a children's invention/engineering club. |
| Primary CTA | "Register interest" appears in the first screen and again near the end. |
| No false production claims | The site does not imply live booking, payment, confirmed policies or all age groups being available unless that is true. |
| Safety visibility | Safety/support information is findable from the nav or by normal scrolling. |
| Mobile completeness | The mobile page contains the same essential story, age information, safety reassurance and CTA. |
| Accessible structure | The page has one clear `h1`, logical heading order, usable links/buttons and labelled form controls. |
| Static prototype boundary | No backend, booking flow, payment flow, authentication or CMS is added. |

## Aesthetic Quality

Aesthetic quality is not just whether the page looks nice. For this site, it means the visual design makes the club feel imaginative, trustworthy and hands-on.

Score each item from 0 to 5.

| Criterion | 5 means | 3 means | 1 means |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| First impression | The page feels immediately warm, inventive and credible. | The page is pleasant but generic. | The page feels confusing, cheap, corporate or childish. |
| Visual hierarchy | The headline, supporting copy and CTA are instantly clear. | Important items are visible but compete. | The eye has no clear path. |
| Image direction | Imagery shows real making, parts, hands, materials or prototypes. | Imagery is relevant but slightly stock-like or decorative. | Imagery feels generic, dark, corporate or unrelated. |
| Colour discipline | Teal, coral, gold and warm paper tones are balanced; accents help scanning. | Colours are acceptable but slightly busy or flat. | Colours feel random, muddy, too corporate, too childish or inaccessible. |
| Typography | Type feels confident, readable and friendly across headings, cards and forms. | Mostly readable, with some awkward scale or spacing. | Text feels cramped, oversized, inconsistent or hard to read. |
| Layout rhythm | Sections have clear spacing, predictable grids and enough breathing room. | Layout is usable but a little dense or repetitive. | Layout feels cluttered, jumpy or visually exhausting. |
| Card and component polish | Cards, buttons, badges and tables feel like one system. | Components mostly match but some feel bolted on. | Components look unrelated. |
| Tactile maker feeling | The design suggests building, testing, materials and invention. | The maker feeling is present but weak. | The site could be for almost any children's club. |

Good aesthetic target:

- Average score at least 4/5.
- No criterion below 3/5.
- The hero should pass a three-second screenshot test: a reviewer shown only the first screen should describe it with words close to "children", "making", "invention", "creative", "engineering" or "workshop".

Specific aesthetic checks:

- No text overlaps or spills out of buttons, cards, nav items or form fields at mobile and desktop widths.
- Main body copy should feel comfortable to read, not like dense report text.
- Decorative icons should support scanning, not become the main message.
- The page should avoid a dark hacker/coding-club mood.
- The page should avoid a polished corporate SaaS mood.
- The page should avoid an overly nursery-like mood for older age groups.

## Practical Usability

Practical usability means parents can complete real review tasks without needing the founder to explain the page.

Run these tasks with at least five representative reviewers if possible.

| Task | Success measure | Target |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Explain the offer | After 60 seconds, reviewer can say what The Invention Club is. | 4/5 reviewers succeed. |
| Spot the difference from coding | Reviewer can explain why this is broader than a coding club. | 4/5 reviewers succeed. |
| Find the age fit | Reviewer can find the relevant age band for a child. | 5/5 reviewers succeed within 30 seconds. |
| Understand what children make | Reviewer can name at least two example activities. | 4/5 reviewers succeed. |
| Trust the structure | Reviewer can explain that there are skills, combinations and invention challenges. | 4/5 reviewers succeed. |
| Find safety reassurance | Reviewer can find safety/support information. | 5/5 reviewers succeed within 30 seconds. |
| Register interest | Reviewer can find the CTA and understand that the current form is prototype-only. | 5/5 reviewers succeed. |

Usability scoring:

| Criterion | Good condition |
| --- | --- |
| Navigation | Main nav labels match parent questions: what, ages, curriculum, projects, safety, FAQ, register. |
| Scannability | A parent can skim headings and cards without reading every paragraph. |
| Copy clarity | Sentences are concrete and parent-facing; curriculum terms are explained before they are used heavily. |
| Cognitive load | The page introduces one idea at a time: offer, difference, session shape, ages, curriculum, examples, safety, action. |
| Mobile use | Cards stack cleanly, buttons are easy to tap, and the nav menu is understandable. |
| Form clarity | The form does not pretend to submit data in the static prototype. |
| Performance feel | The page should load quickly enough on an ordinary connection for a parent not to abandon it. |

Quantitative usability targets:

- Primary CTA visible without scrolling on common desktop viewports.
- Primary CTA reachable within two taps from mobile nav.
- Tap targets should be at least 44px high.
- No section should require horizontal scrolling at 320px width.
- Important parent questions should be answered in headings, card titles or FAQ summaries, not only in long paragraphs.
- The page should still make sense with images disabled, although it may feel less rich.

## Brand Guidelines

These guidelines keep the site recognisable as The Invention Club.

## Brand Essence

The brand promise:

> Children learn to make ideas real.

Supporting messages:

- Not just coding.
- Hands-on creative engineering.
- Practical invention powers.
- Build, test, improve, share.
- Structured enough for parents to trust.
- Open enough for children to feel ownership.

## Voice

The voice should be:

- clear;
- warm;
- practical;
- confident;
- curious;
- honest about what is still a prototype.

Use:

- "Children learn to invent."
- "Make it move."
- "Make it notice."
- "Make it stronger."
- "Try it, test it, change it."
- "Register interest."

Avoid:

- academic curriculum jargon as the lead message;
- exaggerated claims about future achievement;
- startup language such as "disrupt", "revolutionise" or "platform";
- school-marketing language that makes the club feel exam-led;
- implying safety, safeguarding, pricing, dates or booking are confirmed before they are.

## Visual Brand

Core visual ingredients:

- warm paper background;
- teal for trust and structure;
- coral for action and energy;
- gold for invention, highlights and warmth;
- secondary accent colours for skill families only;
- cards and badges as the main interface language;
- workshop imagery showing hands, parts, materials, mechanisms and sketches.

Current palette reference from `styles.css`:

| Token | Value | Role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `--paper` | `#fffdf7` | Main page background. |
| `--warm` | `#f5eee2` | Alternating warm section background. |
| `--teal` | `#09666b` | Primary trust/action structure. |
| `--teal-dark` | `#063d42` | Deep headings and footer. |
| `--coral` | `#f15f45` | Primary CTA and energetic accents. |
| `--gold` | `#f4be38` | Brand mark, highlights and warmth. |

Visual do:

- Use real or realistic making imagery.
- Keep cards simple and readable.
- Let the hero image carry the sense of hands-on making.
- Use colour to help parents understand categories and actions.
- Keep buttons consistent and obvious.

Visual do not:

- Use dark code screens as the main visual identity.
- Use generic corporate stock photography.
- Overuse decorative animations.
- Make the page look like a toy brand for toddlers only.
- Make the page look like a formal school prospectus.

## Logo And Naming

Until a real logo exists, the `IC` mark can act as a placeholder.

Rules:

- Always write the full name as "The Invention Club" in first reference.
- Use "The Invention Club" as the hero `h1`.
- Do not shorten to "TIC" in parent-facing copy.
- The `IC` mark should feel like a simple maker badge, not a final brand identity.

## Content Brand Rules

The site should keep translating the curriculum into parent language.

Preferred pattern:

1. Parent-friendly phrase.
2. Concrete example.
3. Curriculum term only if useful.

Example:

> Children collect practical invention powers, such as making something move, making it notice the world, or making it stronger. In the curriculum, these powers are called skill families.

Avoid leading with:

> The schema contains skill families, power cards, integration cards and invention cards.

## Review Checklist

Before considering a website version good, answer these questions:

- Would a parent understand the offer in one minute?
- Does the first screen make the club feel hands-on?
- Does the page explain why this is broader than coding?
- Are age bands easy to find and compare?
- Are examples concrete enough to imagine a child joining?
- Is the curriculum visible but not overwhelming?
- Does the visual style feel warm, inventive and credible?
- Does the page avoid overpromising launch details?
- Is "Register interest" easy to find?
- Would this still work as a static prototype with no backend?
